E-COIN FIELD GUIDE

Know what you sign.
Own what you learn.

Walk through the protocol, protect your keys, and test your understanding before moving value.

01 / SUPPLY

Finite from genesis.

Exactly 20,000,000 EC are created in block zero and assigned to the Genesis Treasury. Faucet grants and USD-market purchases move existing treasury coins; fees cycle back into treasury. No path mints additional EC.

02 / OWNERSHIP

Keys authorize value.

Your browser creates Ed25519 keypairs. The address identifies the public key; the private key signs a precise transaction. Nodes verify the signature, nonce, timestamp, balance, and fee before accepting it.

03 / SETTLEMENT

Fast, ordered batches.

Transactions enter a bounded mempool, reserve funds immediately, and settle in fee-prioritized batches. Nonces preserve account order while per-sender limits keep block capacity fair.

04 / CONTRACTS

Predictable programs.

Timelocks release once after a deadline. Vesting divides funds into scheduled releases. Hashlocks release to a beneficiary who reveals the committed secret, or refund the creator after expiry. Every transition is replay-verified.

05 / REGULATION

Rules depend on where you are.

Real-world crypto products can trigger money-transmitter, AML/KYC, sanctions, consumer-protection, and tax rules. If a service exchanges value, holds customer funds, or markets an investment, the compliance burden can change quickly by country and state. This app is a devnet sandbox, not legal advice.

06 / SECURITY

Attackers look for shortcuts.

Use unique long passwords, enable multi-factor authentication wherever it exists, verify addresses before sending, and keep recovery copies offline. Never share a seed phrase, never approve urgent surprise requests, and treat any promise of guaranteed profit as a red flag.

07 / OFFLINE SIGNING

Separate signing from broadcast.

Unsigned transfer files contain public transaction intent but no private key. A matching wallet can sign that intent locally, while another online device broadcasts the short-lived signed envelope. Always compare the fingerprint, amount, recipient, fee, and expiry on both devices.

WALLET SAFETY

Three habits beat clever attackers.

  1. Encrypt and back up. Enable the wallet vault, use a unique password, and keep an offline backup. NIST guidance favors long, memorable passwords and discourages brittle rules that make people reuse weak ones.
  2. Verify the destination. Compare the beginning and end of an address, review new-recipient warnings, and treat QR codes or copied addresses as untrusted until checked.
  3. Understand finality. A signed transfer or deployed contract cannot be quietly reversed. Test with a small amount first, and remember that crypto scams often rely on urgency, impersonation, and promises of easy money.
